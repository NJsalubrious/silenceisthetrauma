The Buffer

I

Elizabeth forgot names. She forgot appointments and birthdays and where she left her keys. She could strip a combustion engine down to its crankshaft and reassemble it in under two hours, but she could not remember the name of the man who sold her milk every morning for six years.

She had a physics degree and a PhD in commercial chemistry. She held two patents before she turned thirty-five. She built her own laboratory in the back shed with equipment she sourced from a hospital liquidation sale in Parramatta. She would spend fourteen hours in that shed, goggles pushed up onto her forehead, muttering about polymer decay, her tea going cold on the bench beside her.

Pop met her at a factory where he was fixing a security gate. She was working in the office, filling in shifts to pay for her studies. He was welding. She stopped to watch. He lifted the mask and saw her standing there.

"Going to rain," he said.

"Yes," she said.

"You got an umbrella?"

"No."

He walked her to the bus. He came back the next day and fixed a tap in the kitchen of the house she was renting. She didn't ask him to. She mentioned it once, in passing, while he was welding, and the next afternoon the tap was fixed.

Pop was a big man. Quiet. He had a psychology degree he never talked about and fifteen years working as a counselor in the juvenile justice system. Before that, he grew up on four hundred acres of cattle country. His parents died within a year of each other and the land was sold, split evenly between him and his two sisters. One moved to Perth. The other to Tasmania. They stayed close.

He had money from the sale and he had a pension from the prison system and he needed very little. He fixed things. He read. He kept his opinions to himself unless someone asked, and even then he was careful.

Elizabeth had a son. She had lost custody when the boy was an infant. The circumstances were complicated and she did not talk about them. Pop did not ask. He understood that she carried something and that pushing at it would not help.

She had met the boy's father in a pub where she worked the taps. He was a traveler. He walked into the Royal Hotel wearing linen that looked like it had been slept in on a train, and he spoke with the cadence of a man who had been to places the regulars had only seen on television. He picked up languages quickly. He told stories about mountains in Nepal and markets in North Africa. He had come from a family with money but he had walked away from it. He moved through rooms without disturbing them and people adjusted themselves toward him without knowing why.

Elizabeth was nineteen. She went with him. She traveled for two years. She came back pregnant and alone. The man did not leave with anger or cruelty. He simply continued on, the way a river continues past a bend. She never heard from him again.

Pop was the opposite of that man. He did not tell stories. He stood still. When he said something it had weight because he had thought about it first. He blocked the wind.

II

Elizabeth had been trying to contact the foster parents for several months. She wrote letters. She called. She did not want to disrupt anything. She just wanted to know how the boy was doing. She had the right, she said. Pop agreed, although something in the back of his throat tightened when she talked about it.

The foster parents were vague. They returned one call out of six. They said the boy was fine. They said he was doing well at school. They did not invite Elizabeth to visit.

Then one Saturday in February, the doorbell rang.

Pop was in the garage. Elizabeth was in the back shed. Pop wiped his hands on his jeans and opened the front door.

A boy stood on the step. Fifteen years old. Clean white shirt tucked into dark trousers. No bag. No adult with him. He had walked up the driveway and rung the bell and now he stood there, hands at his sides, looking at Pop with an expression of polite interest.

"Hello," the boy said. "You must be Johna."

Pop looked at him.

"I'm Dominic," the boy said. "Is Elizabeth home?"

Pop called her. Elizabeth came through the house, wiping her hands on a rag, and when she saw him she stopped in the hallway. She made a sound that Pop had never heard from her before. Something between a gasp and a word that didn't form properly. Then she was past him and pulling the boy into her arms.

Dominic hugged her back. He said the right things. He said he understood how hard it had been. He said he had wanted to come for a long time. He was articulate, measured, entirely composed. He listened to everything Elizabeth said and he nodded at the right moments and he asked questions that showed he had thought about her situation with a clarity unusual for a boy his age.

Pop made tea. He sat at the kitchen table and he listened. Dominic ate two pieces of toast. He had good manners. He looked you in the eye when he spoke. He asked Pop about the garage and about the farm where he grew up. He was interested. He remembered details from earlier in the conversation and referenced them later, which made Elizabeth smile because she did the same thing.

He stayed for three hours. He said he would come again. He shook Pop's hand at the door. His palm was dry and his grip was firm and even.

III

Dominic came back the following week. And the week after. Each time without warning. He just appeared at the door.

Within two months he had moved into the spare room. The foster parents did not contest the transfer. They signed the paperwork and said nothing. Elizabeth regained custody.

He was an excellent student. Captain of the mock trial team. Prefect. High marks across every subject. He played sport. He was well-spoken and he was liked by his teachers and he made Elizabeth proud in a way that Pop could see physically changed her. She stood straighter. She laughed more. She left the shed earlier in the evenings.

Pop noticed small things.

Dominic told Elizabeth he had finished his homework. Pop glanced at the desk in the spare room later that evening. The books had not moved. The pens were in the same position. When Elizabeth mentioned it the next morning, Dominic smiled and explained he had done the work at the library. It was a reasonable explanation. Pop would have believed it if he had not checked the desk.

Another time, Dominic told them he had been at a friend's house after school. The school called that afternoon because Dominic had not attended his last two classes. When Pop mentioned it at dinner, Dominic looked confused for a moment, then said the school must have made a mistake, that he had been in the library during free period and the teacher probably hadn't marked the roll. He said it with such steady, casual certainty that Elizabeth accepted it immediately.

Pop accepted it too. Because it could have been true. Each individual explanation could have been true.

IV

When Dominic turned seventeen he started disappearing.

He would leave the house without a bag and return two or three days later and sit at the kitchen table and eat toast. He would not explain where he had been. If Elizabeth asked, he would say he stayed with a mate. He never smelled of alcohol. His clothes were always clean. His hair was combed.

He had money. Pop found three bundles of fifty-dollar notes in the bottom drawer of his dresser when Elizabeth asked him to fetch a hammer. The bundles were held together with thick rubber bands. Pop put the hammer on the bench and closed the drawer. Dominic had a weekend job at the hardware store that paid seven dollars an hour.

He began talking about property. He brought brochures to the dinner table. He suggested Pop and Elizabeth invest in a development in the western suburbs. He had a company name and a contact number. He was seventeen years old.

Pop said no. He said it quietly, at the table, and Dominic accepted it immediately. He folded the brochure and put it in his pocket and asked Elizabeth to pass the salt.

V

A boy at Dominic's school died. He fell from the upper-level walkway outside the science block. The school called it a tragic accident. The circumstances were never fully discussed. The police attended but no charges were laid.

Pop came home that afternoon. Dominic was on the back steps, reading a textbook. He had a green apple on the step beside him, half eaten.

"Bad day at the school," Pop said.

Dominic closed the book slowly and looked up. His face showed concern. It looked correct. His brow pulled in and his mouth set and he shook his head.

"It is," he said. "The railing was rusted. I told Mr. Whelan about it last term."

Pop leaned against the doorframe. "Did you know him?"

"He was in my legal studies class. He wasn't very careful."

Dominic picked up the apple and bit into it. The expression of concern had already left his face. The muscles had relaxed. He chewed and turned the page.

Pop went inside. He stood at the kitchen sink and ran the tap and washed his hands for a long time.

VI

The girl came to the house on a Tuesday. It was raining. She was sixteen years old, wearing her school uniform, and she was pregnant. She stood on the front porch and she couldn't breathe properly. She kept grabbing at the doorframe.

"Is he here?" she said.

Pop went to Dominic's room. The door was open. The wardrobe was bare. The desk was cleared. The drawer where the bundled notes had been was empty. No note. No forwarding address. The bed was made, the pillow squared, the corners tucked.

Elizabeth brought the girl inside. She sat her on the couch and wrapped a towel around her shoulders and made her tea. The girl's name was Sarah. She was in Year 11. She said Dominic told her he was coming back.

Dominic did not come back.

He enrolled in a law degree the following year. He dropped out twelve months later. He did not need the qualification. He was already building something else.

Sarah gave birth to a girl. She named her Ethel. Sarah could not manage. Elizabeth and Pop took the baby.

VII

Ethel grew up in the house. She had Elizabeth's speed. She pulled apart radios. She asked questions faster than anyone could answer them. She misspelled words and skipped steps and understood the spine of things without needing the textbook. Gran—Elizabeth—let her move fast. Pop rotated the tyres on her bike and cleaned the chain and never said a word about it. They just knew.

Dominic's name started appearing in the newspapers. Property developments. Rezoning applications. Council tenders. He was photographed at charity events in the city, wearing expensive suits, shaking hands with ministers. On paper he was a success story. Self-made. Ambitious. Articulate.

Then the letters arrived.

Thick cream envelopes. A family law firm on Martin Place. They requested primary care of the minor, Ethel Ryker. They cited a meaningful relationship and financial stability.

Dominic had not visited the house in years. He had not sent a birthday card. He had not called. The first contact was a legal filing.

VIII

Elizabeth's solicitor withdrew from the case on a Monday morning. He called the house and said he could no longer represent them. He did not explain why. Pop drove to his office. The receptionist said Mr. Davies was unavailable. Pop sat in the waiting room for two hours until Davies came out to use the bathroom. Pop stood up. Davies looked at the carpet. He said a man had approached him at a restaurant and recited his home address and his youngest daughter's school and the name of her ballet teacher. Davies said he was sorry. He went back into his office and closed the door.

The mail stopped arriving on Tuesdays. A man in a grey sedan parked three houses down the street. He had a clipboard. He sat there for four consecutive days.

Ethel's red tricycle disappeared from the backyard. The gate was still latched. Three days later the tricycle was sitting in the middle of the front lawn. The tyres were slashed. The metal frame had been polished until it shone.

IX

Pop sat at the kitchen table with a notebook. Elizabeth stood at the counter. She had a name. A man she had met through her work at the university, years ago. He worked for the Australian Federal Police.

Pop picked up the phone. He did not mention Ethel. He did not mention the tricycle or the solicitor or the man in the sedan. He read out five property development applications he had found in the local council records, filed under three different holding companies. He listed the discrepancies in the zoning boundaries. He gave the tax routing numbers that crossed state lines.

Federal jurisdiction.

He hung up. He drank his tea. Elizabeth sat down across from him. Neither of them spoke for a long time. Outside, the sprinkler clicked and turned and clicked again.

X

They left on a Tuesday morning. Pop backed the station wagon out of the driveway. Ethel sat in the back seat with a plastic doll and a box of books. Elizabeth locked the front door and stood on the step for a moment, looking at the house. Then she got in the car.

They drove north. The concrete gave way to scrub. The scrub gave way to cane. Queensland. A property at the end of a dirt road, bought with the money from Pop's share of the farm. Red soil. Humidity that pressed on the chest. Cicadas in the trees.

Pop turned off the engine. The silence after the motor died was enormous. He opened the door and stepped onto the dirt.

The property was not a hiding place. Dominic had the money and the reach to find anyone on the continent. But the distance was hours of road, and hours translated to time, and time was what they needed. His attention had shifted. The federal investigation had given him other problems to manage. The angling for Ethel had stopped, for now.

Elizabeth set up a laboratory in the shed within three months. Pop built a fence. Ethel rode her bike along the boundary line, cataloguing birds she identified from a field guide she had memorised in a single afternoon.

Pop sat on the veranda in the evenings. He did not talk about Dominic. Elizabeth did not talk about Dominic. They had bought the dirt and they had bought the distance and they waited for whatever came next.
