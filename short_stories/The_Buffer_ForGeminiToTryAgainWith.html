The Buffer

Part I. The Frame

The heat off the bitumen distorted the air above the road, bending the light into a watery mirage. Fifteen-year-old Dominic stood on the front step. No sweat beaded on his forehead. The collar of his school shirt sat crisp against his neck, the tie knotted with aggressive symmetry.

Elizabeth dropped the garden hose. Water pooled by her boots, turning the dry dust to a thick, dark red mud. The hose hissed, a continuous spray soaking the dead grass.

Pop wiped engine grease from his knuckles with a rag. Fifteen years pacing the juvenile wards taught a man to read the set of a jaw, the frequency of a blink, the tension coiled in the shoulders. Dominic’s eyes tracked Elizabeth’s approach. He did not step forward to meet his mother. He waited for the space to close. He required her movement. He demanded the gravity shift to his center.

A boy returns home. A zero-sum transaction.

Elizabeth wrapped her arms around his shoulders. Her fingers dug into the fabric of his shirt. Dominic patted her back. Two light taps. The rhythm of a metronome. A simulation of comfort executed flawlessly by a machine lacking the biological hardware for the emotion.

Part II. The Resonance

Elizabeth secured custody. The foster parents offered no resistance, their absence a silent testimony to a battle they had long ago abandoned. Dominic moved into the spare room. The house absorbed him without a ripple.

He scored top marks. Mock trial captain. Perfect posture at the dinner table. On paper, he represented the apex of adolescent achievement.

Pop watched the hands. At the dinner table, Dominic held his fork with precise rhythm. A steak knife sheared through gristle. No frustration when the meat resisted. Just applied, continuous force until the obstruction yielded.

When baby Ethel cried in the night, a jagged scream piercing the thin walls, Dominic’s door remained shut. A normal teenager complained about the noise. A brother offered to help, or shouted to keep the baby quiet. Dominic simply ceased to exist in the adjacent room. The crying possessed no utility for him. It generated no data he required. He tuned the frequency out entirely.

Part III. The Friction Coefficient

The boy at the school died on a Tuesday. A fall from the upper balcony outside the science block. The principal called it a tragic misstep. The police asked few questions. An accident.

Dominic sat on the back step that afternoon, eating a green apple. The crunch snapped through the humid, oppressive air. The juice coated his lips.

Pop stood by the screen door, the mesh separating them.

"A bad business at the school," Pop said. The words hung in the heat.

Dominic chewed, swallowed. He looked at the apple core. "The railing was substandard rust. The coefficient of friction on the wet concrete was low. The center of mass shifted over the fulcrum. A predictable outcome."

Physics. Mass and velocity. The boy was a variable.

Not a single word about the boy's fear. No mention of the screaming. Just the physics of the fall. The apple cracked again between his teeth.

Part IV. The Eradication

The girl arrived in the middle of a torrential downpour. Sixteen years old. Rain plastered her dark hair to her cheeks. She screamed at the front door, her fingernails clawing at the wood, leaving deep scratches in the varnish.

Elizabeth pulled her inside. The girl’s stomach swelled heavy and hard against the wet fabric of her school uniform. Ethel’s mother. The breathing came in jagged, hyperventilating gasps.

Dominic was gone.

Pop kicked open the bedroom door. The wardrobe hung bare. The textbooks vanished from the desk. He left no note. He left a pregnant teenager sobbing on the linoleum, her fingernails bleeding.

He possessed money now. Bundles of fifty-dollar notes had appeared in his drawer weeks prior, secured with thick rubber bands. He required no support. He erased his own footprint, severing the connection the moment the liability outweighed the asset.

Part V. The Atmospheric Pressure

Years bled out into the soil. Dominic enrolled in a law degree, then vanished from the university rosters. Yet, his name materialized in property development applications, council rezoning approvals, offshore trust registrations.

Ethel grew. She possessed Elizabeth’s velocity, running through the yard, dismantling radios, asking questions at a blistering pace.

Then the letters began.

Not handwritten letters from a brother. Thick, cream-colored envelopes from high-end family law firms in the city center.

Best interests of the minor. Primary care.

The legal phrasing sought custody of Ethel. Dominic required an asset he did not currently possess. An imbalance in his ledger required correction. He did not ask. He initiated a structural acquisition.

Part VI. The Alteration

The siege lacked physical violence. It was a creeping environmental rot, an infection of the mundane.

The mail ceased arriving on Tuesdays. A man in a grey suit parked a Holden three houses down, recording license plates with a clipboard resting on the steering wheel.

Ethel’s red bicycle disappeared from the locked shed. The padlock remained unbroken. Three days later, the bicycle materialized on the front lawn. The tires lay slashed, the rubber ribbons splayed on the grass. The metal frame, however, gleamed. Someone had polished it to a mirror shine.

A stranger approached Elizabeth’s solicitor at a crowded charity dinner. The stranger whispered a specific, unlisted bank account number directly into the solicitor's ear, followed by the address of his daughter's primary school. The solicitor withdrew his representation the next morning via a couriered letter.

The house, once a sanctuary, mutated into a glass jar. The air inside grew stale, recycled, observed.

Part VII. The Strike

They required friction. Financial blocks were insufficient against a man who minted his own currency. Dominic bypassed civil law entirely.

Elizabeth possessed a contact. AFP. A quiet name residing in a secure federal registry.

Pop did not pack bags. He sat at the kitchen table, the landline phone pressed hard against his ear. He fed the contact specific anomalies in the council zoning reports Dominic favored. Small discrepancies. Tax routing errors crossing state lines. Federal jurisdiction.

A pebble in the gears. A grinding halt.

The strike was purely administrative. It placed Dominic’s name in a federal database he could not locally bribe, intimidate, or overwrite. It forced his eye away from the house. It demanded a massive recalculation of his resources to seal the breach.

Part VIII. The Geography of Time

The window opened. A temporary gap in the crushing atmospheric pressure.

The Holden packed tight, the suspension groaning. Tires crunched over gravel, leaving the concrete maze of the western suburbs in the rearview mirror.

They drove north. Queensland. Red dirt and suffocating cane fields. The Buffer property.

It was not a hiding place. A ghost operating a billion-dollar infrastructure could locate anyone on the continent. It was a tactical expanse of land. Distance translated to time. Time translated to survival.

Pop stared through the windshield, his hands gripping the leather wheel. The road stretched out, shimmering in the brutal heat. The threat remained, a low hum in the atmosphere, waiting for the federal friction to clear.

They bought the dirt. They bought the silence. They waited for the storm to pivot.
