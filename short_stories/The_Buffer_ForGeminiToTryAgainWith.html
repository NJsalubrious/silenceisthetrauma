The Buffer

Part I. The Arrival

The bitumen radiated heat, blurring the edge of the front fence. Dominic stood on the concrete step. He was fifteen. His school shirt was buttoned to the collar. His tie lay flat against his chest.

Elizabeth dropped the garden hose. The nozzle hit the dirt. Water pooled around her boots, turning the red clay into thick mud. She ran across the lawn.

Pop stood by the screen door, wiping grease from his hands with a rag. He had spent fifteen years working the juvenile wards. He watched the boy's jaw. He watched his hands. Dominic’s eyes tracked Elizabeth as she crossed the yard. He did not step off the concrete. He did not extend his arms.

Elizabeth pulled him into a hug. Her fingers pressed deep into the fabric of his shirt. Dominic raised his right hand. He patted her upper back. Tap. Tap.

Part II. The Spare Room

The foster parents signed the paperwork without requesting a meeting. Dominic unpacked a single duffel bag into the spare room.

At dinner, he ate the roast lamb. He held the knife in his right hand, the fork in his left. When the knife hit a knot of gristle, he did not saw at it or push the plate away. He applied steady downward pressure until the blade cut through to the ceramic.

Ethel was four months old. She woke at 2:00 AM, screaming. The walls of the fibrous-cement house were thin. Pop sat up in bed, his feet finding the cold floorboards. He walked down the hall. He passed Dominic’s door. The gap beneath the door was dark. No sound came from inside. No movement, no sigh of frustration, no shifting of bedsheets. Just total silence.

Part III. The Fall

A boy from the high school fell from the second-story walkway on a Tuesday. The principal called the house to say classes were suspended.

Dominic sat on the back step that afternoon, eating a green apple. He bit into the skin. A sharp snap.

Pop leaned against the doorframe.

"Bad business at the school," Pop said.

Dominic chewed the piece of apple. He looked down at the core in his hand. "The railing was rusted. The concrete was wet from the rain."

He took another bite.

Part IV. The Departure

The girl knocked on the front door in the middle of a downpour. She was sixteen, wearing the local high school uniform. Her hair stuck to her cheeks. When Elizabeth opened the door, the girl grabbed the doorframe. Her knuckles were white. Her stomach bulged against the wet fabric of her skirt.

Pop walked into Dominic's room. The wardrobe doors were open. The hangers were bare. The drawers were pulled out. No textbooks on the desk. No note on the pillow.

Three bundles of fifty-dollar notes had sat in the bottom drawer a month prior, held together with thick rubber bands. The drawer was now bare wood.

In the hallway, the girl was sobbing, her breath catching in her throat, leaving bloody streaks on the doorframe where she had scraped her nails.

Part V. The Letters

Dominic did not return. Two years passed. He enrolled in a law degree in the city, then dropped off the roster.

Ethel was three. She ran through the sprinkler in the backyard, screaming with laughter, her bare feet slapping the mud.

The mail arrived on Thursday. Elizabeth opened a thick, cream-colored envelope. The letterhead belonged to a family law firm located on Martin Place.

Pop read the text over her shoulder. It requested primary care of the minor, Ethel Ryker. It cited a meaningful relationship and financial stability.

Dominic had not visited the house in three years. He had not sent a birthday card. He had not made a phone call.

Part VI. The Silence

The following Tuesday, the postie did not stop at their letterbox. He rode his motorbike past the house, accelerating.

A grey Holden sedan parked three houses down the street. The driver wore a suit. He sat in the driver's seat with a clipboard, writing. He did not turn the engine off.

Ethel’s red tricycle was left by the back steps on Wednesday evening. On Thursday morning, it was gone. The yard was undisturbed. On Sunday, the tricycle sat in the exact center of the front lawn. The rubber tires were slashed, peeled back from the rims. The metal frame had been polished. It reflected the morning sun perfectly.

Elizabeth’s solicitor called on Monday. He said a man had approached him at a restaurant the night before. The man had recited the solicitor’s home address and the name of his youngest daughter's ballet teacher. The solicitor said he could no longer represent them.

Part VII. The Call

Pop did not yell. He sat at the kitchen table. He pulled a notebook from his pocket.

Elizabeth poured a cup of tea. She had a name from her time at the university labs. A man who worked for the Australian Federal Police. A contact for reporting specific chemical discrepancies.

Pop picked up the landline phone. He dialed the number. He did not ask for police protection. He did not mention the tricycle.

He read out five property development applications he had found in the local council records, filed under three different holding companies. He listed the discrepancies in the zoning boundaries and the tax routing numbers.

He hung up the phone. He drank the tea. It was lukewarm.

Part VIII. The Road

Pop backed the station wagon out of the driveway. The trunk was packed with suitcases. Ethel sat in the back seat, holding a plastic doll.

They drove north, out of the western suburbs, onto the highway. The concrete gave way to scrub, then to cane fields.

Queensland. A property at the end of a dirt road. Red soil. Humidity that sat thick on the chest.

Pop turned the engine off. The silence was absolute. Only the sound of the cicadas in the trees. He did not look at the rear-view mirror. Distance was time.

He opened the car door and stepped onto the dirt.
